General Introduction

Boston is a dynamic city known for its many college campuses. As a result, the demographics change drastically as college students come and go throughout the year. These changes affect the general demographics and environment of the city, but we are looking to discover how the changes in the time of year affect crime throughout the city. By examining the location, time, and offense of a crime and comparing it with different neighborhoods, we can discover how different features affect crime in the city. By allowing students and Boston residents to examine crime in their neighborhoods, they can better understand the safety of where they live and how they may be affected by the changing seasons.

Data Introduction

For our analysis we will be utilizing a dataset found on the Boston Police Data Hub and is free to download for anyone online. The data is broken down into 21 features and approximately 212,000 rows. Some of these features are extractions from others, as the column for month data of a crime can also be found in the column containing date-time objects for crimes. However, most of the columns do contain useful/unique information. The majority of the data is categorical in nature, with the main outlier being datetime. This is largely due to the nature of crime data, as the focus is mainly on frequency which is not a continuous datapoint. A major reason we are using this dataset is also for its location information. The dataset includes the zip code, street address, and district for a vast majority of the points. This will allow us to create interesting spatial graphics later on.

D3 Chart

We also studied which days of the week most commonly result in crimes. This bar chart shows the number of crimes committed broken down by each day of the week. As demonstrated on the chart, crimes occur most frequently on Friday as a total of 32,000 crimes were committed on Friday. To contrast, approximately 28,000 crimes occured on Sunday, the day when the lowest number of crimes were committed. Interestingly, Saturday is not on the lower or higher end of the spectrum which suggests that weekends vs weekdays have no impact on the amount of crimes committed. Rather the day of the week as an independent value is the most significant factor.

Circle interactive chart

In this last polar bar chart, we are examining the difference between violent and property crimes month by month. The data for this plot was narrowed down to the years after 2020 as this category was not collected/input into the data regularly before then. We can see that in general property crimes are taking place at a much higher rate month over month(Avg ~1.5k for property, .7k for violent). Also, we can continue to observe the trend of more crimes being committed in summer months within both property and violent crime categories

Conclusion

In summary, we found that larger areas in Boston, particularly Dorchester, Roxbury, and Downtown (with Chinatown having notably fewer crimes), tend to experience more crime, which is relevant given their proximity to Northeastern campus. We noticed that more crimes occur during the summer months, even when college is out. Verbal assault was the most common crime type, followed by incidents involving motor vehicles and vandalism. These findings emphasize the need to understand crime patterns for improving safety measures in urban areas like Boston.
